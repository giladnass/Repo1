# AI Strategy and Digital Operating Models

## Summary

This chapter introduces the transformative impact of artificial intelligence and digital networks on business, arguing that AI is becoming the universal "runtime" or operational foundation for companies. The authors explain how digital operating models enable three critical advantages—scale (infinite replicability at near-zero marginal cost), scope (easy connectivity across activities), and learning (continuous improvement through data)—that traditional operating models cannot match. Through detailed case studies of Amazon, Walmart, and Tencent/WeChat, the chapter illustrates how AI-driven firms are fundamentally different: they improve with growth rather than suffering from complexity costs, creating new competitive dynamics beyond traditional disruption theory. The authors position this transformation as affecting every industry and sector, not just tech companies, establishing a framework for understanding how strategy, leadership, and organizational structure must evolve in the age of AI.

## Key Findings

- AI is becoming the operational foundation of business (the 'runtime') where software and algorithms shape the critical path of execution, displacing human activity from core processes in ways that transform the very concept of the firm.
- Digital operating models provide three advantages over traditional models: infinite scalability at near-zero marginal cost, expanded scope through connectivity to complementary activities, and embedded learning that improves automatically through data processing.
- Companies like Amazon and Tencent demonstrate that digital systems scale more easily and improve rather than degrade as they grow, eliminating the complexity costs that traditionally constrained firm size and scope.
- Traditional disruption theory is insufficient for understanding AI-driven transformation—digital firms don't just compete with better products, they operate with fundamentally different operating models that reshape entire industries and create new competitive dynamics.
- The economic transformation is universal, affecting every industry and segment—not just 'new economy' companies—requiring all organizations to rethink how they create, capture, and deliver value.
- The unbounded scale and scope of AI-driven firms create new risks (privacy, cybersecurity, bias, fake news) that existing regulatory and ethical frameworks are not designed to address.

## Questions Raised

- How should regulators adapt antitrust and competition policy to address digital firms whose market power stems from network effects and data advantages rather than traditional measures of market share?
- What new approaches to leadership, governance, and ethics are required when organizational impact scales without traditional constraints on growth, scope, and learning?
- Can traditional companies successfully transform by rearchitecting their operating models, or does the collision between digital and analog operating models inevitably favor digital-native firms?
- How should society balance the consumer benefits of AI-driven personalization, convenience, and efficiency against the dislocations and vulnerabilities created by embedding increasing portions of economic and social life in digital networks?
