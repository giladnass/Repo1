# ADHD Clinical Reference Guide

## Summary

This document provides healthcare professionals with comprehensive, up-to-date information on ADHD, covering epidemiology, pathophysiology, diagnostic and clinical trial scales, and biostatistical methods. It highlights increasing ADHD prevalence across age groups, common comorbidities, neurobiological mechanisms involving brain structure/function and neurotransmitters, and tools for assessing symptoms and treatment outcomes. The guide emphasizes practical applications of scales for diagnosis and research, alongside statistical interpretation of clinical data.

## Key Findings

- ADHD prevalence is rising, with 9.4% of U.S. children aged 2-17 diagnosed in 2016, and 4.4-5.2% of adults aged 18-44 affected.
- ADHD is associated with structural/functional brain differences in regions like the prefrontal cortex, striatum, and cerebellum, alongside dopamine, norepinephrine, and glutamate dysregulation.
- Comorbid psychiatric conditions are highly prevalent, with 80% of adults and 50% of children/adolescents with ADHD having at least one additional disorder.
- Numerous validated scales (e.g., ADHD-RS-5, ASRS, Conners) exist for diagnosing ADHD across age groups, with distinct applications for children, adolescents, and adults.
- Clinical trial design and statistical interpretation require attention to effect sizes, confidence intervals, and biases to assess treatment efficacy and safety accurately.
