# Mental Models Physics and Chemistry

## Summary

This document introduces the mental model of Relativity, emphasizing the subjectivity of perspective and its implications for understanding reality. Through examples from physics (Galileo's ship experiment, Einstein's theory of relativity) and real-world scenarios (eyewitness testimonies, historical events), it argues that multiple perspectives are inherent to human experience. The text advocates for perspective-taking and thought experiments to reduce cognitive biases and enhance decision-making by integrating diverse viewpoints.

## Key Findings

- Perspective is inherently limited and subjective, influenced by one's frame of reference (e.g., Galileo's moving ship experiment).
- Thought experiments (like Einstein's lightning strikes) demonstrate that events can be perceived differently depending on the observer's motion, challenging absolute notions of time and simultaneity.
- Relativity in human perception explains contradictions in eyewitness accounts and underscores the importance of cross-verifying information.
- Integrating multiple perspectives reduces blind spots, though biases and memory distortions complicate this process.
- Historical examples (e.g., *Rashomon*, Minh Hong trial) illustrate how conflicting narratives arise from individual vantage points.

## Questions Raised

- How can individuals systematically mitigate the impact of cognitive biases when integrating multiple perspectives?
- What practical methods exist for applying relativistic thinking to resolve conflicts or improve collaborative decision-making?
- To what extent do cultural or societal norms shape the ability to adopt alternative perspectives?
