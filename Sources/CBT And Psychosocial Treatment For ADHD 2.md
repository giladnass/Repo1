# Cbt And Psychosocial Treatment For Adhd

## Summary

The document outlines the application of Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for ADHD across developmental stages, emphasizing its role in teaching compensatory skills not addressed by medication. It details structured CBT modules for organizing, managing distractibility, and cognitive restructuring, supported by meta-analyses and clinical trials showing efficacy in adults and preliminary success in adolescents. The MGH treatment program integrates multimodal interventions, with evidence suggesting CBT reduces ADHD symptoms, improves functioning, and addresses comorbid conditions.

## Key Findings

- CBT complements medication by teaching compensatory skills like organization and problem-solving, which medications alone do not address.
- Structured CBT modules (e.g., task prioritization, problem-solving) show measurable symptom reduction and functional improvement in adults, with promising results in adolescents.
- Meta-analyses (e.g., Knouse et al., 2017) demonstrate moderate to large effect sizes for CBT in reducing ADHD symptoms and improving self-reported functioning.
- Studies (e.g., Safren et al., 2010; Sprich et al., 2016) confirm CBT's efficacy in medication-treated adults and adolescents, with sustained benefits at follow-up.
- Cognitive restructuring addresses dysfunctional thoughts (e.g., catastrophizing) linked to emotional and behavioral impairments in ADHD.

## Questions Raised

- How effective is CBT for adolescents not on ADHD medications, and how does it compare to active control groups?
- What are the long-term sustainability and generalizability of CBT outcomes across diverse populations?
- How can CBT modules be optimized for younger children (preschool/school age) with limited executive functioning?
- What role do family/significant others play in enhancing CBT adherence and outcomes for adolescents?
