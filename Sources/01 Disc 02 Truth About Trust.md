# Integrity and Competence in Trust

## Summary

The transcript explores the multifaceted nature of trust, arguing that traditional research has overemphasized integrity while neglecting competence. It highlights how trust involves both intentional honesty and the ability to deliver results, supported by psychological studies on unconscious competence assessment. The discussion also addresses how contextual factors, biases, and evolutionary pressures shape trust decisions, often outside conscious awareness.

## Key Findings

- Trust requires evaluating both integrity (intentions) and competence (ability), not just one aspect.
- Humans rapidly assess competence through nonverbal cues like posture and gestures, often unconsciously.
- Trust calculations are influenced by context, rewards, and subconscious biases, making predictions complex.
- Evolutionary pressures for social cooperation may have driven the development of advanced trust-processing in the brain.
- Self-trust is inherently uncertain due to future self-bias and mental inconsistencies over time.

## Questions Raised

- How can individuals balance trust in integrity versus competence in different relationships?
- What methods exist to improve conscious awareness of unconscious trust cues?
- How do cultural differences affect the weighting of integrity vs. competence in trust?
- What practical strategies can mitigate the risks of subconscious trust biases?
