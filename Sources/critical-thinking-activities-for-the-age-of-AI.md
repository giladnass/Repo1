# Critical Thinking Activities For The AI Age

## Summary

This practical guide by Dr. Med Kharbach presents 13 hands-on classroom activities designed to transform AI interactions from passive answer-seeking into active critical thinking exercises. The author synthesizes emerging research on AI's cognitive impacts—including cognitive offloading, metacognitive laziness, and the homogenization of thought—while noting limitations in current studies. Rather than banning AI, the guide argues for using AI as a "training ground" for skeptical engagement, with each activity mapped to Robert Ennis's framework of six critical thinking skills. The activities range from 10-30 minutes, work across disciplines, and emphasize that reflective debriefing after each exercise is where lasting thinking habits form.

## Key Findings

- Research shows students who rely on AI for cognitive tasks exhibit reduced critical thinking engagement, diminished metacognitive monitoring, and 'thinking atrophy'—but study limitations include narrow populations and lack of longitudinal data
- The default student pattern of 'prompt, receive, accept, move on' is where critical thinking goes quiet; the solution is inserting friction that requires questioning, evaluation, and personal reasoning
- All 13 activities map to Ennis's six critical thinking competencies: analyzing arguments, judging source credibility, handling ambiguity, evaluating assumptions, recognizing fallacies, and asking clarification questions
- AI will always give an answer—the critical question is whether that answer is worth anything without proper context and verification
- Fluency in AI-generated text does not equal accuracy; AI can argue opposing positions with equal confidence, revealing that confident-sounding prose may lack genuine depth or evidence
- The reflective memo following each activity is essential—the activity opens the door, but reflection is where students internalize critical thinking habits

## Questions Raised

- How will longitudinal studies reshape our understanding of AI's cognitive impacts as the technology matures?
- If AI can mislead experts on topics they know deeply, how can novices recognize distortions in unfamiliar material?
- Do people evaluate content differently based on who they believe produced it, and what biases does this reveal?
- How can educators effectively scale critical AI literacy interventions across diverse institutional contexts and student populations?
